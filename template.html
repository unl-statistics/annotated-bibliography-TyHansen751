Annotated Bibliography: Deep Learning from a Statistical Perspective

  • Ian J. Goodfellow, Jonathon Shlens, and Christian Szegedy “Explaining and Harnessing Adversarial Examples” (arXiv, March 2015), https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.1412.6572.

    • Summary: This paper investigates adversarial examples—inputs to neural networks that are intentionally perturbed in small, often imperceptible ways to cause misclassification with high confidence. The authors argue that the primary vulnerability of neural networks to adversarial perturbations is not their nonlinearity or overfitting, but rather their linear nature. They provide quantitative evidence for this explanation and show that adversarial examples generalize across different network architectures and training sets. The paper also introduces the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM), a simple and computationally efficient approach for generating adversarial examples, and demonstrates that adversarial training can reduce test error.

    • Reliability assessment: The authors are highly credible researchers in deep learning and computer vision. Ian Goodfellow is known for inventing Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), Jonathon Shlens is a research scientist at Google, and Christian Szegedy has made significant contributions to computer vision. Published in ICLR 2015 (International Conference on Learning Representations), one of the premier venues for deep learning research, this paper has been extremely influential with over 20,000 citations. In the Annual Review article, this paper is cited in the section on “Interpretability, Causality, Fairness, and Trustworthiness” as foundational work on adversarial examples—a critical topic for understanding the reliability and security of deep neural networks. The phenomenon of adversarial examples reveals fundamental limitations of DNNs that are essential for understanding their trustworthiness in real-world applications.

    • Missing information:

      • To fully understand why linearity causes vulnerability to adversarial perturbations, one would need a deeper understanding of how high-dimensional geometry affects neural network decision boundaries. The paper’s argument relies on understanding how small perturbations in many dimensions can accumulate into large changes in the output, which requires background in linear algebra and high-dimensional spaces.
      • The paper discusses adversarial training as a defense mechanism, but understanding the full landscape of adversarial defenses and attacks would require knowledge of the extensive subsequent research on this topic. The paper was an early contribution, and the field has evolved significantly with new attack methods (like PGD attacks, C&W attacks) and defense strategies that build on or challenge the ideas presented here.
  • Yann Le Cun “Learning Process in an Asymmetric Threshold Network,” in Disordered Systems and Biological Organization, ed. E. Bienenstock, F. Fogelman Soulié, and G. Weisbuch (Berlin, Heidelberg: Springer, 1986), 233–40, https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-642-82657-3_24.

    • Summary: This paper presents an early formulation of the backpropagation learning algorithm for asymmetric threshold networks with hidden units. Le Cun addresses a fundamental limitation of single-layer networks: they can only learn linearly separable functions, which severely restricts their computational power. The paper introduces a learning procedure that allows networks with “hidden units” (intermediate layers without direct environmental feedback) to learn more complex, non-linearly separable functions. The algorithm adjusts connection weights to minimize the difference between actual and desired outputs by propagating error information backward through the network layers, enabling the hidden units to develop useful internal representations.

    • Reliability assessment: Yann Le Cun is one of the pioneers of deep learning and convolutional neural networks, winning the Turing Award in 2018 (along with Hinton and Bengio) for breakthroughs in deep learning. This 1986 paper, published in the Springer proceedings “Disordered Systems and Biological Organization,” represents early work on backpropagation developed independently around the same time as Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams’ famous Nature paper. The Annual Review article cites this work in Section 2.2 when discussing the historical development of backpropagation, noting that gradient-based optimization using backpropagation has been fundamental to neural network training since the 1980s. This paper is directly relevant to understanding how the backpropagation algorithm emerged and why hidden units were such an important conceptual breakthrough.

    • Missing information:

      • To fully understand the mathematical formulation, one needs background in gradient descent optimization and the chain rule for computing derivatives through composed functions. The paper discusses weight updates and error propagation but assumes familiarity with these optimization concepts.
      • Understanding the significance of this work requires historical context about the limitations of perceptrons and why hidden units were considered problematic before backpropagation. The paper references threshold functions and linearly separable functions, concepts from early neural network theory that require background knowledge to appreciate why they were limiting factors in the field’s development.
